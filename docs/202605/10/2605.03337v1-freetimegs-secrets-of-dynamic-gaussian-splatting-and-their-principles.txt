Abstract. The recent surge in 4D Gaussian Splatting (4DGS) has achieved impressive dynamic scene reconstruction. While these meth-ods demonstrate remarkable performance, the specific drivers behind such gains remain less explored, making a systematic understanding of the underlying principles challenging. In this paper, we perform a comprehensive analysis of these hidden factors to provide a clearer per-spective on the 4DGS framework. We first establish a controlled baseline, FreeTimeGS ours , by formalizing and reproducing the heuristics of the state-of-the-art FreeTimeGS. Using this framework, we dissect 4DGS along its fundamental axes and uncover key secrets , including the emergent temporal partitioning driven by Gaussian durations and the discrepancy between photometric fidelity and spatiotemporal consistency. Based on these insights, we propose FreeTimeGS ++ , a principled method that em-ploys gated marginalization and neural velocity fields to achieve superior stability and robust dynamic representations. Our approach yields re-producible results with reduced run-to-run variance. We will release our implementation to provide a reliable foundation for future 4DGS research.

Existing approaches to dynamic Gaussian splatting adopt several different formulations. Some methods deform persis-tent 3D Gaussians through deformation networks [5, 10, 16, 18, 19, 24, 27, 32], while others model temporal dynamics directly within Gaussian primitives using time-dependent parameters or spacetime features [11, 14, 17, 26, 29, 30]. 2 Related Work 

Dynamic Scene Reconstruction. Dynamic scene reconstruction has tradi-tionally been studied through implicit neural representations. Early dynamic radiance field methods extend NeRF [20] by modeling time through canonical-space deformations [21 –23], scene-flow parameterizations [15], or temporal latent codes [21]. While these approaches offer high expressiveness, they typically require complex training schedules and dense sampling, making large-scale or real-time applications challenging. The introduction of explicit neural representations significantly improved efficiency and rendering speed. 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [7] proposed a differentiable splatting method with adaptive densification and pruning, enabling real-time, high-quality novel view synthesis. Building upon this foundation, a growing body of work extends Gaussian primitives into the temporal domain to model dynamic scenes. One prominent direction maintains canonical 3D Gaussians and learns time-dependent deformation fields [1, 10, 16, 24, 27]. These approaches employ defor-mation networks to predict motion offsets for Gaussian primitives, sometimes augmented with additional representations such as per-primitive embeddings [1]. Related efforts also explore distillation-based pipelines that transfer dynamic features into Gaussian primitives [10]. Alternatively, several methods embed temporal dynamics directly within Gaussian primitives. These include intrinsic 4D parameterizations with time-conditioned attributes [30], spacetime Gaussian feature splatting [14], compact parametric trajectory modeling [11], sparse timestamp interpolation schemes [12], and duration-based activation mechanisms that allow primitives to appear and disappear over time [26]. Although these methods share the same differentiable 4 L. Y. Lee et al. 

splatting framework, they adopt diverse assumptions regarding primitive lifespan, temporal continuity, and density control. 

Physical Consistency in Dynamic Modeling. Although dynamic Gaus-sian splatting primarily focuses on photorealistic reconstruction, several works introduce structural constraints to improve motion coherence. Geometry-aware deformation strategies incorporate spatial consistency priors to preserve local structure during motion [19]. Local rigidity or neighborhood consistency constraints have also been explored to regularize deformation fields and reduce excessive non-rigid distortion [17, 28]. Some methods further impose explicit rigidity priors such as ARAP-style constraints to maintain local geometric structure during dynamic deformation [5]. In addition, motion-aware frameworks leverage external motion cues such as optical flow to align Gaussian deformation with observed image-space motion, providing additional supervision for dynamic reconstruction [3, 4, 32]. These approaches demonstrate that incorporating motion-related priors can help promote more coherent dynamic behavior beyond purely photometric super-vision. 

Optimization Stability and Reproducibility. Beyond representation and motion design, dynamic Gaussian splatting heavily depends on density control mechanisms and training heuristics. Adaptive densification and pruning intro-duced in 3DGS [7] remain fundamental in dynamic extensions [11, 27, 30]. Addi-tional strategies include progressive training and backtracking [12], error-guided refinement [14], sliding-window optimization [24], cross-temporal consistency modeling [6], and context-aware deformation learning [25]. These components substantially influence convergence behavior and reconstruction stability. Motivated by these observations, we conduct a controlled analysis of represen-tation, motion modeling, and density control to better understand the structural behavior of dynamic Gaussian reconstruction.

3.2 Underspecified Components in FreeTimeGS ours .

While FreeTimeGS Wang outlines the core 4D representation, reproducing its performance requires explicit formalization of initialization, optimization, and relocation pipelines. Here, we detail the specific mechanisms implemented in FreeTimeGS ours .

Spatiotemporal Initialization Pipeline. A critical factor for 4DGS perfor-mance is the density and quality of the initial point cloud. While FreeTimeGS Wang 

suggests using RoMa [2] for 2D matching, we formalize this loosely defined pipeline into a robust framework. For Geometric Feature Matching , we use RoMa but maintain efficiency by matching each view with only its k-nearest cameras. Geometric validity is enforced during triangulation through cheirality and coordinate-range checks. The temporal density is governed by a Keyframe Stride . Through technical correspondence for faithful reproduction, we identified that setting the stride to 10 for DyNeRF and 1 for SelfCap recovers the reported performance. For velocity initialization, we estimate the initial motion vector v using a k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) search between point clouds from adjacent keyframes, and then optimize it jointly during training with Adam under the same exponential learning-rate schedule used for other parameters.

4 Secrets Explored 

While recent 4DGS methods have demonstrated exceptional performance in high-fidelity reconstruction, the underlying principles driving these gains are often entangled with the characteristics of commonly used benchmarks. Commonly used datasets such as DyNeRF [13], SelfCap [29], and other multi-view indoor sequences typically feature a structured environment comprising static camera arrays, stationary backgrounds, and localized dynamic subjects. 4.1 Representation Temporal opacity. Time-varying opacity is a fundamental mechanism for representing dynamic scenes in 4D Gaussian Splatting, operating alongside explicit Gaussian motion. In FreeTimeGS-style representations, this temporal degree of freedom provides a simple yet expressive way to control when each primitive contributes to the rendered sequence. We therefore analyze how the learned temporal scales of Gaussians relate to their functional roles in the reconstructed scene. 

Stratified distribution of durations. We characterize each Gaussian by its learned temporal scale relative to the full sequence span of the video. After convergence, this distribution exhibits a strongly stratified structure. As shown in the histograms in Fig. 1(a), a substantial fraction of Gaussians concentrates near the full sequence span, while the remaining Gaussians form a broader distribution of shorter-scale primitives. This separation suggests that the learned representation does not allocate temporal support uniformly across primitives, but instead organizes them into groups with distinct temporal roles. 

Implicit temporal partitioning. To examine the functional roles of these groups, we visualize the scene after partitioning Gaussians by their temporal scales. In Fig. 1(b), we color-code Gaussians by temporal scale, where cooler colors indicate shorter-scale primitives and warmer colors indicate longer-scale primitives. Motion consistency. Dynamic Gaussian methods differ in how they represent motion, ranging from canonical-deformation fields to explicit trajectories and native spacetime primitives. However, they are typically evaluated using image-space rendering metrics such as PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS. These metrics measure image fidelity but do not directly evaluate whether the learned representation provides temporally consistent correspondences or motion estimates. 

Photometric-motion decoupling. Using FreeTimeGS ours , we instantiate this diagnostic with explicit per-Gaussian velocities, which make the learned motion directly visualizable. As shown in Fig. 2(a,b), the rendered RGB image can appear visually clean, while the corresponding velocity visualization exhibits high-frequency variation and motion leakage in regions that are largely static. This does not mean that the rendering is poor; rather, it shows that photometric supervision can admit solutions whose explicit motion is not uniquely determined by appearance alone. A possible mechanism behind this behavior is role switching. Instead of ex-plaining a moving surface through a single coherent trajectory, the representation can rely on different short-lived Gaussians that become active at nearby times. In Fig. 2(c), we visualize such activated short-lived Gaussians at the same time step, suggesting that some dynamic content may be explained through time-local primitive activations rather than through a persistent motion track. We use this as a diagnostic case study rather than as a claim about all 4DGS formulations. 

Diagnostic implication. These observations suggest that rendering metrics alone do not fully characterize dynamic Gaussian representations. A more com-plete evaluation should also consider motion induced by the learned representa-tion, such as image-space flow, static-region motion leakage, track consistency, or long-term 3D trajectory consistency. 4.2 Initialization 

Since 4DGS optimizes a high-dimensional spatiotemporal representation, the initial support provided in space and time can materially influence both con-vergence and final reconstruction quality. We therefore examine initialization as a diagnostic factor along two axes: spatial correspondence assumptions and temporal sampling density. 

Spatial initialization. For spatial initialization, we follow FreeTimeGS Wang and use RoMA [2] to obtain dense correspondences for triangulation. Our observations suggest that the key issue is not simply whether a newer matcher is used, but whether its implicit or explicit scale assumptions are aligned with the geometry of the scene. In particular, several sequences in DyNeRF and SelfCap combine locally enclosed structure with visually unbounded regions such as long corridors or background windows. In these cases, choosing an outdoor-style scale prior in RoMA proved important for recovering stable depth in the extended regions. By contrast, replacing this setup with a newer matcher that lacks an equivalent scale prior degraded geometry and reduced rendering quality in those areas. We do not treat this as a benchmark claim about matchers themselves; rather, it indicates that spatial initialization in 4DGS is sensitive to scene-scale assumptions, and that architectural upgrades alone do not guarantee better downstream behavior.

4.3 Training Density Control. The training dynamics of 4DGS are strongly influenced by density control, which reuses inactive Gaussian primitives by relocating them to more informative regions. During training, primitives whose original opacity falls below a visibility threshold ( σ ≤ τopacity ) are classified as part of the dead set . Rather than permanently discarding them, we sample new target locations from the active population according to primitive opacity and view-space gradient magnitudes, encouraging relocation toward regions with high rendering contributions. 

Relocation Policies and Parameter Inheritance. We find that relocation behavior is governed not only by when relocation is performed, but more impor-tantly by how parameters are inherited from the sampled target primitive. In particular, the treatment of opacity and scale has a major impact on the resulting reconstruction. We compare three policies. Partial Copy (dead ) copies all target parameters except opacity and scale, which are preserved from the dead primi-tive. Exact Copy (target ) strictly duplicates all target parameters, including opacity and scale. MCMC (3d_mcmc ) follows MCMC-based density control [8], recomputing opacity and scale to provide a more balanced redistribution. 

Optimization Dynamics and Artifact Accumulation. Our experiments show that inheritance rules strongly influence the accumulation of background FreeTimeGS ++ 11 

artifacts. As shown in Tab. 4, Exact Copy achieves the highest PSNR but masks severe qualitative degradation. By fully cloning high-contribution targets, redundant primitives accumulate in the same spatial regions, leading to exces-sive rendering contributions. Without explicit regularization, this accumulation amplifies fine haze-like wobbling artifacts in static backgrounds to fit per-frame view dependencies. Conversely, Partial Copy yields the lowest PSNR. Moreover, this policy results in a high proportion of inactive primitives (i.e., a high dead ratio where final primitives satisfy σ ≤ τopacity ). Because these relocated primitives inherit near-zero opacities, they remain close to visibility threshold, manifesting as subtle flickering noise over repeated optimization cycles. Ultimately, MCMC provides the most visually stable rendering by redis-tributing opacity and scale to account for the number of cloned primitives, effectively suppressing over-amplification. Space-time (X-t) slices from the Self-Cap bike1 sequence (Fig. 3) visually validate this by comparing the rendered results with the ground-truth video. While Exact Copy produces gray noise and haze-like wobbling artifacts, and Partial Copy exhibits unstable jittering patterns, MCMC closely matches the ground truth, yielding straight and stable boundaries.

L = λ1L1 + λsLssim + λlLlpips + Lreg + Lgate + Lcc + Ldistill , (5) where Ldistill stabilizes the NVF during warmup, and Lcc regularizes the color correction parameters. This principled framework balances photometric fidelity with physical plausibility and numerical stability.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we have provided a systematic perspective on the secrets behind the success of 4D Gaussian Splatting (4DGS). By analyzing the impressive performance of state-of-the-art methods like FreeTimeGS through our formalized baseline, we identified key drivers such as emergent temporal partitioning and the critical role of spatiotemporal initialization. Our approach not only achieves competitive photometric fidelity but also significantly improves physical motion plausibility and optimization repeatability through affine color correction.
