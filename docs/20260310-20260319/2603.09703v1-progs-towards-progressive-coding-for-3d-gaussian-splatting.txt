# ProGS: Towards Progressive Coding for 3D Gaussian Splatting 

Zhiye Tang, Lingzhuo Liu, Shengjie Jiao, Qiudan Zhang, Member, IEEE, Junhui Hou, Senior Member, IEEE, 

You Yang, Senior Member, IEEE, Xu Wang, Member, IEEE 

Abstract —With the emergence of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), numerous pioneering efforts have been made to address the effective compression issue of massive 3DGS data. 3DGS offers an efficient and scalable representation of 3D scenes by utilizing learnable 3D Gaussians, but the large size of the generated data has posed significant challenges for storage and transmission. Existing methods, however, have been limited by their inability to support progressive coding, a crucial feature in streaming applications with varying bandwidth. To tackle this limitation, this paper introduce a novel approach that organizes 3DGS data into an octree structure, enabling efficient progressive coding. The proposed ProGS is a streaming-friendly codec that facilitates progressive coding for 3D Gaussian splatting, and significantly improves both compression efficiency and visual fidelity. The proposed method incorporates mutual information enhancement mechanisms to mitigate structural redundancy, leveraging the relevance between nodes in the octree hierarchy. By adapting the octree structure and dynamically adjusting the anchor nodes, ProGS ensures scalable data compression without compromising the rendering quality. ProGS achieves a remarkable 45× reduction in file storage compared to the original 3DGS format, while simultaneously improving visual performance by over 10%. This demonstrates that ProGS can provide a robust solution for real-time applications with varying network conditions. 

Index Terms —Volumetric Scene Streaming, 3D Gaussian Splat-ting, Deep Reinforcement Learning

Unlike neural radiance fields (NeRF) [3], which rely on dense neural networks to represent a 3D scene, 3DGS explicitly represents each scene as a collection of learnable 3D Gaussians. Gaussians are visualized as ellipsoids in the 3D space, each characterized by learnable parameters such as pose, shape, and color. In the rendering process, these 3D Gaussians are “splatted” onto an imaging plane corresponding to a particular viewpoint, and the final image is synthesized through α-blending [5]. This splatting-based rendering process simplifies and accelerates scene rendering by avoiding the frequent and computationally expensive ray sampling operations required by methods like NeRF [3]. Furthermore, it facilitates high-quality, differentiable rasterization. However, this approach is not without its challenges. While 3DGS enables real-time rendering, it leads to high memory and storage consumption due to the large number of 3D Gaussians (often millions) required to represent a single scene. As a result, there is a critical need to develop efficient compression strategies for 3DGS that reduce its data volume without sacrificing rendering quality. Recent advancements aim to address this challenge by exploring data structure optimization and the use of hierarchical encoding techniques, such as octrees, to reduce storage overhead while maintaining scene fidelity. With the unorganized and sparse nature of 3DGS, it is chal-lenging to achieve efficient compression of 3D Gaussians [2], [6]. These difficulties stem from the inherently high memory requirements and the lack of structural organization in 3DGS, which typically results in a large amount of redundant data. In response, numerous approaches have been proposed to tackle this issue from various perspectives. Some methods introduce pruning techniques, such as applying masks to discard 3D Gaussians with low utility, thereby reducing the overall data size [7]–[9]. Additionally, codebook-based quantization meth-ods have been explored, aiming to further compress the data by grouping similar 3D Gaussians into predefined sets, thus improving storage efficiency [10]. Despite their effectiveness, these methods focus primarily on directly compressing the original 3DGS format, and they often overlook the potential to leverage the structural relationships between Gaussians. The lack of such relational awareness limits the opportunity for creating even more compact representations. Building on this, Scaffold-GS [11] took inspiration from grid-guided NeRF [12] and proposed a more structured ap-proach. This method replaced 3D Gaussians with learnable anchors, initialized from SfM point clouds and aligned to a voxel grid. During both training and rendering, these anchors dynamically generate surrounding neural 3D Gaussians via multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), significantly reducing mem-ory usage. This anchor-based framework has been shown to provide more efficient representations. More recently, further

Progressive coding, on the other hand, allows for more flexible data transmission, where coarse-grained data is first transmitted and refined with finer details, improving channel efficiency and visual quality as the stream progresses. To push the boundaries of streaming-friendly 3DGS repre-sentation, this work draws inspiration from the point cloud domain, specifically contemplating the idea of representing 3D space using a tree structure [14]. The proposed solution is ProGS , a streaming-friendly codec designed to facilitate Pro gressive coding for 3D Gaussian 

Splatting. In particular, following the anchor-based design of Scaffold-GS [11], ProGS mimics the octree structure and divides the anchor collection into multiple levels of detail (LoDs), ensuring that each level progressively refines the scene representation. During the training process, ProGS adaptively grows and prunes the branches of the octrees, dynamically adjusting the resolution of each part of the scene, which reduces memory usage and optimizes the encoding efficiency. However, such a LoD structure faces inherent challenges. Lower LoDs represent incomplete 3D scenes, resulting in visual degradation of the rendered images. To overcome this, ProGS hypothesize that the representation goals of low and high LoDs are aligned, and thus, it is affirmative to improve the performance of lower-level anchors by enhancing the mutual information (MI) [15] between parent and child nodes within the octree. By enhancing the common information, lower-level anchors can more accurately represent the scene’s details, significantly improving the progressive coding performance. Furthermore, building on the work of HAC [13], ProGS also utilizes context-based encoding techniques, where each an-chor’s context is queried from its location and passed through arithmetic coding (AE) [16], ensuring the generation of a compact binary bitstream. This approach not only ensures that the 3DGS data is compactly represented but also guarantees efficient, progressive decoding and reconstruction for stream-ing applications. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: 1) A progressive codec for 3DGS: ProGS is a codec that facilitates progressive coding for 3DGS by constructing volumetric scenes as hierarchical octree structures. This novel approach not only allows for efficient encoding and streaming of 3DGS data but also enables adaptive bitrate transmission, where the scene’s details can be progressively refined during the decoding process based on available bandwidth. 2) MI enhancement for low LoDs: To address the chal-lenge of visual quality degradation due to the incomplete representation of 3D scenes at lower LoDs, MI enhance-ment mechanisms are introduced. By leveraging the common information shared between parent and child nodes in the octree, ProGS is able to enhance the ability of lower-level anchors to more accurately represent the full 3D scene. This process significantly improves the rendering quality of lower LoDs, mitigating the loss of detail and ensuring smoother transitions as the scene is progressively refined. 3) Coarse-to-fine optimization: To further optimize the compression efficiency of ProGS, a coarse-to-fine op-timization scheme is proposed. This method facilitates the reduction of data redundancy and promotes MI be-tween anchors, enabling a more compact representation. By adjusting the anchor placement dynamically during training, ProGS achieves a balanced representation that preserves high-fidelity details in areas of interest while minimizing the storage requirements for less critical regions of the scene. 4) Extensive experimental validation: Extensive experi-ments demonstrate that ProGS achieves comparable or superior fidelity compared to state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods. Remarkably, ProGS can reduce the number of anchors used for scene representation by up to 67%, yet still maintain nearly 80% of the visual perfor-mance. These results highlight ProGS’s ability to achieve substantial compression without sacrificing rendering quality, making it a promising solution for real-time 3

A. Compression for 3D Gaussian Splatting 

3DGS [1] has emerged as an innovative technique for repre-senting 3D scenes, offering efficient ways to capture complex details within volumetric spaces by utilizing anisotropic 3D Gaussians. These Gaussians, which represent scene elements as ellipsoids with learnable poses, shapes, and colors, provide a compact representation that enables high-quality rendering through differentiable rasterization. However, a significant challenge arises from the large storage cost of explicitly recording each Gaussian, as each one occupies up to 248 bytes, necessitating high-efficiency compression techniques to handle large-scale 3DGS data. The existing 3DGS compression approaches can be broadly categorized into two main types: primitive-based methods 

and anchor-based methods . Primitive-based methods focus on directly compressing the 3D Gaussian primitives used in the original 3DGS format. For example, Lee et al. [7] intro-duced pruning techniques to remove redundant and less in-formative Gaussians, thereby reducing memory requirements. Similarly, approaches like those in [8], [9], [17] utilized vector quantization methods to group similar Gaussians into codebooks for more efficient encoding, while LocoGS [18] decomposed Gaussian attributes into explicit and implicit parts, which also effectively reduced information redundancy. To address the non-structural nature of the original 3DGS format, CompGS [19] proposed a hybrid primitive structure that leverages the predictive relationships between Gaussians to improve compression. MesonGS [20] borrowed the oc-tree voxelization and region-adaptive hierarchical transform (RAHT) [21] from point cloud compression, also achieved promising performance. More recently, Chen et al. proposed FCGS [22], which is a plug-and-play codec for any pre-trained 3D Gaussian scenes, pushing the boundaries of 3DGS coding. These methods, while successful in reducing the size of the 3DGS representation, often fail to exploit the underlying structural relationships between the Gaussians, which could lead to even more efficient compressions. On the other hand, anchor-based approaches aim to re-place the original 3D Gaussians with anchors, which are more compact and can represent multiple surrounding Gaussians. Scaffold-GS [11] was one of the first to introduce this ap-proach, using anchor-centered features to represent 3D scenes. Each anchor is initialized from a SfM point cloud and aligned to a voxel grid. During both training and rendering, anchors generate a set of surrounding neural 3D Gaussians via MLPs, thus saving considerable memory and storage. More recently, HAC [13] further advanced anchor-based compression by using a context-based hash-grid assisted coding framework, which incorporates adaptive quantization techniques to en-hance compression efficiency. This framework achieved SOTA performance in 3DGS compression, significantly reducing data size while maintaining high visual fidelity. Although these approaches have made significant strides in reducing the storage and memory requirements of 3DGS representations, they primarily focus on single-bitrate sce-narios, where a fixed amount of data is compressed and transmitted. This approach does not effectively handle the variability in available network bandwidth, especially for real-time streaming applications where bitrate adaptation is cru-cial. These methods generally encode 3D scenes into static files, which limits their flexibility and scalability for dynamic transmission. Progressive coding, on the other hand, allows for scenes to be progressively decoded and refined over time, enabling higher channel efficiency and better quality as more data becomes available. This paper introduces ProGS, a novel codec designed specifically for progressive coding of 3DGS. ProGS organizes scenes into hierarchical octree-like structures, which are adaptively adjusted during training to support pro-gressive refinement. This method not only achieves efficient compression but also ensures that the rendering quality is not compromised during progressive decoding, marking asignificant advancement over previous methods. 

B. Progressive Coding 

For now, progressive coding has not yet been extensively explored for 3DGS, but it has attracted considerable attention in the fields of image, video, and point cloud compression. Some progressive codecs, such as traditional JPEG [23], JPEG2000 [24] and recent [25] for images, as well as H.264/SVC [26] and SHVC [27] for video, typically construct enhanced layers for media content. These codecs enable the adaptive selection and decoding of data based on the current network conditions and the device capabilities, allowing for efficient streaming and rendering across varying bandwidths. This adaptive nature makes progressive coding highly valuable for multimedia content delivery, where high-quality visuals are prioritized, but only available bandwidth is used. In par-allel, learning-based approaches have also gained popularity. Notably, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) have been em-ployed in various works [28]–[33], capturing representations at lower bitrates and gradually refining them to generate high-resolution images. More recently, the adoption of trit-plane coding methods, as seen in [34], [35], has yielded comparable gains, particularly in image and video compression. Mao et al. [36] leveraged priors from a generative adverseral network (GAN) [37], also improved upon traditional methods. Further-more, Dasari et al. [38] and Lin et al. [39] have contributed novel scalable approaches for video coding, significantly en-hancing the scalability and efficiency of video codecs for modern applications. However, unlike the regular and structured nature of images and videos, point clouds represent 3D data as discrete point sets that are unevenly distributed across a three-dimensional space. This inherent irregularity makes it particularly challeng-ing to apply progressive coding to point clouds. G-PCC [40] was a pioneering effort that introduced scalability for point cloud compression, utilizing the octree structure and LoD schemes to improve data representation and compression. While these methods have made strides in improving point cloud compression, they still face challenges in handling implicit attributes of 3DGS scenes, such as co-variance and anchor features. These attributes are particularly sensitive to prediction errors, which can degrade the quality of the resulting 3D scene representation. Therefore, a more refined approach for progressive coding should involve the LoD partitioning strategy during scene training processes and incorporate context-based entropy coding to ensure lossless compression. Among the efforts in this direction, Octree-GS [43] aimed to incorporate multi-resolution anchors to achieve LoD-structured 3DGS. However, despite these efforts, the approach fell short in providing sufficiently refined representations for 3D scenes, primarily due to the lack of entropy coding for data reduction. This omission resulted in limited performance when compared to other methods in the field. In contrast, this paper proposes ProGS , which models 3DGS scenes as octrees and incorporates MI enhancement to improve compression efficiency. By utilizing the relationship between parent and child nodes within the octree, ProGS enables better scene representation at lower LoDs, facilitating efficient progressive coding and improving both compression and rendering qual-ity. III. M ETHODOLOGY 

The framework of ProGS is illustrated in Fig. 2. Specifically, ProGS begins by constructing an initial octree structure from the input SfM point cloud, where the nodes in the octree correspond to anchors, each associated with a set of attributes 

A, including feature vectors, scaling factors, and offsets. These anchors serve as the foundational elements for predicting surrounding neural Gaussian clusters, which represent the scene’s details at varying levels of granularity. The anchors are distributed at different depths within the octree, and each level corresponds to a voxel region in the 3D space, where the gran-ularity of the representation increases with each subsequent LoD. The l-th LoD of ProGS contains all anchors from level 

1 through to level l, with higher LoDs offering progressively finer resolutions of the scene. During the training process, ProGS employs an adaptive anchor adjustment strategy that dynamically grows and prunes the octree branches based on optimization gradients. This ensures that the octree structure is both compact and flexible, accurately reflecting the scene’s most significant features while minimizing redundancy. To assist with this dynamic adjustment, ProGS utilizes a hash-grid H, which is queried to estimate the distribution of anchor attributes and facilitate adaptive quantization. The quantized attributes, or their decoded counterparts in evaluation mode, are then used to generate neural Gaussian clusters, which are further employed in multi-level differentiable rendering. This process is crucial for ensuring that each level of the octree contributes to the final rendered image, as shown in Fig. 2. In addition, to enhance the visual quality of lower-level anchors, ProGS incorporates the information noise contrastive estimation (InfoNCE) loss [44], denoted as LNCE , along with a coarse-to-fine optimization loss Lc2f for MI enhancement. These mechanisms help improve the representation of the scene at lower LoDs by promoting consistency between parent and child nodes in the octree. A more detailed discussion of the MI enhancement approach is provided in Sec. III-D.

B. Octree Construction 

A well-defined LoD partitioning scheme is crucial for en-abling efficient progressive coding. Octree-GS [43] introduced multi-resolution anchors to represent voxel regions at varying granularities, incorporating a gradient-guided anchor adjust-ment strategy. While this approach allows for multi-resolution representation, their proposed strategy is position-decoupled ,which leads to a loosely organized structure. This decoupling fails to fully exploit the interdependencies between anchors at different levels, resulting in an inefficient representation of the volumetric scene. To overcome this limitation, this work presents a more structured approach in ProGS. In the proposed pipeline, ProGS first estimates an approximate bounding box based on the inputted SfM point cloud P , which provides a sparse yet informative representation of the 3D scene. This bounding box serves as the foundation for constructing the hierarchical octree structure. E. Context-based Entropy Coding

F. Training and Coding 

In the whole training pipeline, ProGS constructs the overall loss by combining the supervisory and regularization terms discussed above so as to jointly optimize fidelity, compactness, and progressive consistency: 

L = Lc2f + λvol Lvol + λNCE LNCE + λe Le, (17) with the entropy-related term normalized per encoded symbol as

(18) Here, Lc2f enforces cross-level supervision so that lower LoDs learn to approximate higher-level renderings, Lvol penalizes the total volume of all generated Gaussians to discourage oversized primitives and promote sparsity, LNCE enhances inter-level MI by contrasting parent–child anchors against negatives, and Le jointly encourages low-entropy, context-predictable attributes and a well-conditioned hash context. The scalar weights λvol , λNCE , and λe control the trade-offs among geometric compactness, MI promotion, and rate; in particular, sweeping λe yields different operating points along the rate–distortion curve. The denominator N  Df +Ds +Do

normalizes by the number of symbols (anchors) and attribute dimensionality to keep Le scale-invariant across scenes and settings. For practical encoding/decoding, we adopt a header–chunk pipeline that aligns with progressive transmission. First, the hash-grid H is encoded/decoded once via AE/AD together with the parameters of the lightweight MLPs that perform context-driven distribution estimation and quantization; this header is cached and reused for all subsequent levels. After-ward, decoding proceeds in a topologically valid order from root to leaves: at each level, the context queried from H

conditions the probability models used by MLP d and MLP q

to decode (or encode) the anchor attributes. The coordinates of first-level anchors are stored verbatim to seed the hier-archy, whereas each subsequent-level anchor is represented compactly by 23 bit, in which 20 bits record the index of its unique parent in the previous level and 3 bits encode the octant offset relative to its parent. Therefore, the absolute positions are reconstructed deterministically from the voxel grid. This design keeps the header amortized, enables per-level independent decoding, and ensures that higher LoDs can be appended incrementally without reprocessing earlier data, which is essential for low-latency progressive rendering. A. Implementation Details 

ProGS is implemented on top of the HAC framework [13] using the PyTorch library [60], and all models are trained on an Ubuntu 20.04 LTS server equipped with an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. Unless otherwise specified, the attribute dimensions are fixed as Df = 32 , Ds = 6 , and Do = 10 

for the anchor feature, scaling, and offsets, respectively. The maximum number of LoDs is set to L = 5 with a base depth lb = 11 , which defines the voxelization granularity for octree construction. For the anchor adjustment policy, the gradient-based growing threshold uses hyperparameters 

β = 0 .01 and τg = 5 ×10 −5, while the pruning threshold on accumulated opacity is τo = 0 .5; adjustment is triggered every T = 100 iterations. For the InfoNCE objective, ProGS constructs Nneg = 100 negatives per anchor and adopt a temperature of τNCE = 0 .03 . F. Execution Time 

With the additional GPU computation required to strictly maintain the octree properties and the multi-level rendering induced by Lc2f , the end-to-end training of ProGS is approx-imately 3× longer than Scaffold-GS [11]. In contrast, the coding path remains lightweight thanks to the LoD-structured design: each level is encoded/decoded only once and can be streamed or cached independently. The per-level encoding and decoding time measured on GPU is summarized in Tab. II; here, the “Header” denotes the one-off payload containing the hash-grid H and the parameters of the small MLPs used for distribution estimation, quantization, and Gaussian generation, which is parsed a single time and reused for all subsequent levels. After the header is loaded, decoding proceeds in a root-to-leaf order with entropy decoding of anchor attributes and on-the-fly Gaussian generation, so the marginal latency of adding an LoD is modest and largely proportional to the number of active anchors at that depth. Rendering throughput (FPS) for each LoD is also reported in Tab. II, showing real-time rates across all levels: coarse LoDs achieve higher FPS due to fewer anchors, while deeper LoDs introduce a gradual reduction in speed yet remain in the real-time regime, aided by anchor pruning and visibility/opacity culling. Overall, despite the heavier training stage, ProGS delivers short per-level coding time and consistent real-time rendering; its progressive chunking enables low-latency scene startup and incremental refinement without re-encoding, which is crucial for interactive viewing and adaptive streaming. V. C ONCLUSION 

This paper presents ProGS, a novel codec designed to facilitate progressive coding for 3DGS. ProGS addresses the challenges associated with efficiently compressing large 3D scene representations while preserving rendering quality, making it suitable for real-time streaming applications with varying network conditions. By leveraging hierarchical oc-tree structures and introducing adaptive anchor adjustments, ProGS enables scalable and efficient compression of 3DGS data. Moreover, this work also introduces MI enhancement techniques, such as the InfoNCE loss and coarse-to-fine opti-mization, to improve the visual quality of lower-level anchors and ensure high-fidelity scene representations even at lower levels of detail. Extensive experiments demonstrate that ProGS achieves superior performance, with up to 45× reduction in storage requirements and over 10% improvement in visual quality compared to existing SOTA methods. ProGS is the first progressive coding framework for 3DGS and offers a promising solution for real-time 3D scene rendering, providing a balance between efficient data compression and high-quality rendering. Future work could explore further optimization strategies for ProGS, including real-time adaptive encoding and decoding mechanisms that can dynamically adjust based on available resources and bandwidth, further enhancing its applicability in diverse scenarios.
